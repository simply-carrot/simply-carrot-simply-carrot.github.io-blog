Why Thomas More Still Matters in an Age of Moral Flexibility

February 2025 · Religion & Law

As a first-year law student who is in the process of converting to Catholicism, I find myself returning again and again to the figure of St. Thomas More. The more I read about him—not just in A Man for All Seasons, but in his own letters—the more his life exposes the thinness of so much modern talk about “following one’s conscience.”

More did not treat conscience as a free-floating feeling, but as a judgment rooted in objective moral truth and the teaching of the Church. That is precisely why his stand against Henry VIII was so costly. If conscience is simply preference, it can always be negotiated or managed. If conscience is an encounter with truth, it becomes something you are willing to die for.

In law school, we are trained to separate our “professional” selves from our deepest convictions—often in the name of neutrality or pluralism. Yet More’s life suggests that the great danger is not that Christians will impose their faith on others, but that we will quietly bracket it off, treating it as irrelevant to questions of justice, authority, and the common good.

For someone like me, standing at the threshold of the Church, More’s martyrdom is both intimidating and strangely consoling. It reminds me that the Catholic faith is not a lifestyle accessory I can bolt onto an existing career plan. It is a claim about what is ultimately real—and about the kind of person I am called to become, even if that eventually puts me at odds with the powerful.

In an age that prizes moral flexibility, Thomas More still matters because he shows us what fidelity looks like when every worldly incentive points the other way. The law needs that kind of courage again. So do I.
